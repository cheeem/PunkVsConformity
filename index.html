Punk

A subculture's evolution, shaped by subversion

From its inception to the modern day, punk has found itself under numerous sounds, aesthetics, values, and identities.

What defines punk rock is subversion - rejecting norms and trends present throughout wider society and within the genre.

Guy Picciotto of Fugazi hanging from a basketball hoop in a Philadelphia YMCA, 1988

Origins

A 1970s Disruption That Shocked Rock Forever

Emerging in the mid 1970s, punk rock quickly identified itself as a polarizing genre. A reaction to the commercialization of rock, punk rose as a subculture that rejected the sterilized product the rock had become.

From its debut, punk rock was sloppy and simple. Anyone could play; all that seperated you from the artist was dedication.

The Ramones

Ian Dickson
Punk promised to build a scene which could not be taken. Its anger, pleasures, and ugliness were to go beyond what capitalism and bourgeois society could swallow. It would be untouchable, undesirable, unmanageable
Roger Hutchings

DIY

Anti-commercialism in Punk

Seeking to escape the commercialization that had stolen rock's soul, punks from the genre's inception adopted a defining do-it-yourself mindset.

An early punk poster for a Dead Kennedys show, 1979

Johan Kugelberg Punk Collection
Feeling excluded and alienated from "the industry", punk youth began setting up their own shows, booking their own tours, publishing their own music media, screening their own shirts, and forming their own record labels
Henry Rollins (2004) famously chronicled his time on the road with Black Flag, detailing the band's commitment to playing small venues across the US, even if that meant going hungry, being spat upon by the audience, and sleeping on couches or in the van.

A newspaper add for Dischord Records, a punk-owned record label

Early DIY record labels - including SST (Black Flag), Alternative Tentacles (Dead Kennedys), and Dischord (Minor Threat), all of which survive today - helped punk spread and reinforced the irrelevance of major labels.

Fashion

Or Lack Thereof

With a decades long reign of commercial fashion and beauty naturally came a rebellion in punk.

Outfits emerged - cheaply refashioned, crudely constructed, and shockingly distressed - a direct response to the newly purchased pristine look promoted by 1970s fashion industry.

London's Punk Fashion of the 1970s

Anarchy UK
Both anarcho and crust punks emphatically express their anti-capitalist politics, as well as rejecting beauty norms and commercial punk fashions via their heavily stitched and patched dark clothing, dreadlocks, or "crust mullets."

Improvised piercings were made with safety pins, razors, hooks, and chains

The release of Minor Threat's In My Eyes EP in 1981 signified the birth of a new movement in punk.

Throwing away the image of a rockstar and the drug-fueled lifestyle of their punk predecesors, straight edge preached sobriety.

The romantic "live fast, die young" mantra was increasingly becoming a grim reality, as many punks piled hard drugs on top of abundant booze, glue, and weed.

A black X drawn on a hand, a common safeguard used by venues to prevent underage concertgoers from ordering from their bars, was adopted as a defining symbol of the Straight Edge movement.

Revolution

Summer

A Rebellion Against Violence

Seeking the revitalize Washington D.C.'s hardcore scene, Dischord Records led by an impassioned Ian MacKaye began a revolution.

An evolution of the Straight Edge manifesto, Revolution Summer promoted veganism and non-violence in punk, discouraging moshing - a defining element of live hardcore.

The movement is also credited with the rise of emotional hardcore, producing bands such as Rites of Spring and Embrace.

Revolution Summer, whose energy and idealism still echo in far corners of the globe, was an idea hatched by D.C. punk Amy Pickering, who had grown dissatisfied with the punk scene's growing violence, creative stasis and social and political apathy.

Rites of Spring

Bert Queiroz
There was a situation where the shows were becoming increasingly, moronically violent, and a lot of people were like: fuck it, I'll drop out, I don't want to be a part of this any more.
This simultaneous challenge to the subculture and the wider world included new musical styles, an opposition to “slam-dancing” and skinhead gang violence, and a critique of the sexism of the scene.
[Revolution Summer] embraced confrontational, creative protest, animal rights, vegetarianism and communal living.

Emotional

Hardcore

The Rise of First Wave Emo

In a genre and world unwilling to explore emotion, punk observed the birth of a new shade of punk.

Driven by introspective lyrics and depressive themes, emotional hardcore, or emo, subverted a society and music scene that - by the 1980s - widely frowned upon emotional vulnerability.

A New

Look

1990s-2000s

As a new generation of young blood found itself injected into punk's veins with the turn of the decade, the genre soon found itself at a crossroads, both sonically and socially.

Therefore, with the rise of the 1990s brought with it an onslaught of new musical evolutions as the genre sought both invited crossover and matured, and social movements seeking to break free of the pre-established dogmas that permeated the hardcore punk.

Metalcore

An Alliance Between Past Enemies

During their height in the 1980s, punk and metal stood as factions seperated by a hateful feud. An intermixing of the two was sure to leave no one happy.

With the release of Suicidal Tendencies' self titled LP, a bridge between the two sworn enemies began to form - a rebellion against punk's own anti-metal dogma. By the 1990s, metalcore solidied itself as the dominant subgenre in hardcore, a title it holds to this day.

Post

Hardcore

Brutality, Matured

Building on hardcore's simplistic blueprint, a collection of bands began to chart new directions for the genre, preforming their very own litnus test expand punk's sonic palette.

Rooted in the 1980s work of bands such as The Minutemen and Hüsker Dü and inspired by the 1988 release of Fugazi's "Waiting Room", post hardcore emerged in the '90s as a rejection of punk's all-to-similar sound in pursuit of creativity.

Riot

Grrrl

Feminism Finds a Home In Punk

Rooted in the rise of 3rd wave feminism, Riot Grrrl sought not only to give voice to victims of sexual abuse and challenge the institutions that attempted to silence them, but to bring female faces to a male-dominated punk scene.

Riot Grrrl flipped the image of an all-guys punk band on its head, all the while subverting wider societal norms of "feminine beauty" that Riot Grrrl artists saw as imposed in search of an aesthetic liberation on their terms.

Dazed
Female punks addressed the "boyocracy" of a DC punk scene that tended to reproduce the male dominance of the world it claimed to reject
